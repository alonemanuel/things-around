i love you nissan — about

back

When I first opened my Facebook account I liked the official page of Nissan, the Japanese multinational automobile manufacturer.

To be precise, I "became a fan" of the page, as it was then called. If I recall correctly, I remember seeing a few posts by the page. Naturally, since the digital realestate was scarce, the algorithm had to sacrifice posts by Nissan to make room for other posts — bands I liked, movies I watched, football players I admired.

15 years later — a few months ago, I decided to tidy my cluttered Facebook feed so I can get more relevant content. I unfollowed dozens of pages that were no longer interesting for me.

Suddenly, I started getting posts by Nissan in my feed. I couldn't really remember when and why I liked their page. It was probably because of my dad's red Nissan Micra that I loved since I was a kid.

Posts about new cars, greetings for Christmas and for Hannuka and other corporational ads, I got them all. Soon I started noticing a curious pattern. The page had 22 Million likes (and thus followers), and yet each post got an average of 4 comments and 30 likes. The top posts could get 210 likes, but I don't think I ever saw one that had more than 8 comments. Some of them were left uncommented.

This is approximately a 0.00000136363% of interaction. To put this in perspective, a person with 800 friends on Facebook that gets an average of 2 comments on his posts, has 0.0025% of comment interaction. If Nissan had 0.0025% of interaction, each post would get an average of 55,000 comments.

This is a homage to those commenters part of the 0.00000136363%, a community built around Nissan Ltd., the Japanese multinational automobile manufacturer.
